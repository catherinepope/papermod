If you have a disability or certain conditions, you might need to request some adaptations for your viva. I’m not going to attempt to cover all scenarios here, but will provide some suggestions to help you identify your specific needs.

## In-Person Vivas

If your viva is in-person, you might need to ensure the location is accessible for you. Be explicit when making the arrangements. Your institution should know if you’re visually impaired or a wheelchair user and make the necessary adjustments. But if you’re visually impaired or a wheelchair user, you’ll probably have ample experience of this not happening. Don’t leave anything to chance. You don’t want the stress of last-minute changes because nobody thought to check your notes.

Explain any accessibility requirements when you submit your thesis, and again when you’re notified of the viva date. When communicating your needs, quote legislation, guidance, and best practice. This can appear more authoritative and it also reminds the institution of their responsibilities. For example, “As stated in the Equality Act 2010, the following adaptations will help ensure my viva is conducted fairly.”

### Physical Disabilities

Depending on your needs, you might need to address the following:

#### Environment

- Good lighting — makes it easier to lip read and identify facial expressions.
- Access to room — is there a lift and a wide enough door?
- Access to a socket — vital for screenreaders or other assistive devices.
- Enough space —  e.g. for a wheelchair, scribe, interpreter, or guide dog.
- A Plan B — if a lift is unexpectedly out-of-order on the day, is there an alternative ground floor location?

#### Examiners

Your examiners might need to adapt their approach, for example:

- Willingness to repeat questions.
- Ensuring only one person is speaking at a time.
- Using clear language with no reliance on non-verbal information.

Much of this might appear obvious, but it won’t necessarily have occurred to examiners with no experience of accessibility needs.

### Neurodivergence

There are many types of neurodivergence, but I’ll focus on two of the most common here: Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC) and Attention Deficit (Hyperactivity) Disorder (ADHD)[1], partly because there’s some overlap between those and many other neurotypes. This is broad, and, of course, it depends on your individual needs. However, you might need to consider the following:

#### Environment

- A quiet environment where you’re less likely to be interrupted or distracted by noise.
- A familiar space, or one that you can view in advance.
- A quiet space for waiting before the viva.

#### Format

- Breaks for decompression.
- Information in advance about the format. As examiners usually meet just before your viva, you won’t get much advance notice. However, you could request that they explain the format at the beginning (this doesn’t always happen).
- Extra time to write down questions if you have difficulty with processing verbal questions.

#### Examiners

Depending on the candidate’s needs, examiners should:

- Allow more time for answering questions.
- Accept that candidate might be overly detailed, ignoring the big picture, unless the question is clearly structured.
- Understand that answers might be jumbled. Shorter, focused questions can help some candidates structure a better response.
- Appreciation that the candidate might be experiencing additional anxiety, beyond what would normally be expected.

**A note on eye contact**

Some autistic people find sustained eye contact overwhelming (it’s also considered rude in some cultures). If this is the case for you, I’d suggest at least engaging eye contact right at the beginning of the viva, during the introductions. Then you can look mainly at your notes or adopt a thinking pose (gazing upwards, or to the corner of the room). Although even brief eye contact might be uncomfortable (as an autistic person, I understand), it’s important to establish this rapport at the outset. Appearing “rude”, even though there’s a very good reason for it, can get the viva off to an awkward start.

If you’re neurotypical, you’d be forgiven for scanning the list above and concluding that this would be helpful for you, too. Yes! Addressing accessibility helps everyone, which is why it’s so frustrating that it’s sometimes seen as an unwelcome faff or an unaffordable luxury. 

### Mental health issues

Perhaps you’re experiencing mental health challenges that are exacerbated by the viva. If you suffer from anxiety, avoid (if you can) an overly aggressive examiner. In this case, their personality is much more important than their expertise.

There are also some steps you can take before the viva to keep yourself calmer. I’ll be going into a lot more detail about this later. For now, reassure yourself that you can prepare for it. 

## Disclosing a Condition

Unfortunately, requesting adaptations (even entirely reasonable ones) obliges you to disclose your condition. Your institution is most likely to respond appropriately if you cite a condition covered by the Equality Act 2010. [LINK] 

Examiners might be unfamiliar with your condition, which means you’re obliged to communicate your needs clearly to everyone involved. Although the examiners should be helping you feel at ease, you might need to do this for them. You’re the expert in your condition.

Perhaps there’s an advocate at your institution who can communicate on your behalf? Or maybe one of your supervisors would be prepared to liaise with everyone involved?

If your institution is unable to make the necessary adaptations for an in-person viva (or you don’t feel confident in their ability to do so), you might consider an online viva.


## Online Vivas

Much of the advice above also applies to online vivas. Most candidates will benefit from minimal distractions, clear audio, and well-lit examiners. If you’re reliant on lip-reading or facial expressions, it’s important that your examiners place their webcams appropriately. Some people inexplicably position themselves so you can see only the top of their head, like they’re sat in a hole. You can explain this in advance, but also be prepared to remind examiners at the beginning of the viva.

If you’re using any special equipment (e.g. a screenreader) that affects the speed or format of your answers, you should also explain this at the beginning. Otherwise, the examiners might think there’s somebody whispering the answers in your ear.

Platforms such as MS Teams and Zoom now provide live transcripts in a range of languages. Although this feature is usually activated by default, you might need to ensure it hasn’t been disabled. Before the viva gets underway, test any features on which you’re relying.

## Top Tips

- Provide plenty of notice — communicate your needs to your supervisor (whichever one is arranging your viva) and the examinations office when you submit your thesis and remind everyone again when confirming the viva date.
- Be explicit — don’t just explain your condition, tell them explicitly what adaptations are required.
- Double-check — where possible, check for yourself, or ask a trusted colleague, e.g. whether a room is really wheelchair accessible.
- Consider what’s in your Circle of Control — later, you’ll start planning the day and identifying how you can make it easier for yourself.
- Don’t take it personally — if your needs aren’t met, it’s not malicious. Nor is it a reflection of you or your research. Be prepared to advocate or find someone to do this on your behalf. Your examiners have an obligation to treat you fairly, but they might need some help in doing so.

## Conclusion

Explain that no university will change the nature of the viva, e.g. giving you the questions in advance. Like any exam, you can request extra time, but you can't ask for the paper.
